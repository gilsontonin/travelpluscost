# Brand strategy — transparency is the moat

The winning thesis. We will never out-inventory or out-spend Expedia/Booking. The **one** axis we can
win is the thing they **structurally cannot copy** without blowing up their own economics: **honesty
about price.** Their growth depends on opaque markups, personalized/surveillance pricing, sold placement,
and drip fees. We are the antidote. Read with `docs/POSITIONING.md` (the legal guardrails).

---

## 1. The wedge — be the antidote to the category's villain
Most travelers don't *know* the tricks are happening. Our job: **make the invisible visible**, then be
the obvious alternative. The villain isn't a competitor — it's a set of industry practices:
- prices that change by device / location / history (surveillance pricing)
- a hidden markup you can't see
- "sponsored" results — placement sold to the highest bidder
- drip pricing — resort/service fees revealed at the end

> Like DuckDuckGo to Google's tracking, or Costco to retail markup: we win by being the trustworthy
> opposite, and incumbents can't match it without indicting themselves.

## 2. The Transparency Charter (our public, ownable commitments)
A signed, public pledge — the brand artifact a big OTA can't co-sign. Each is **exactly true** for us:
1. **Same price for everyone** — never changed by your device, location, or history.
2. **One flat fee, in plain sight** — not a hidden percentage markup.
3. **All-in pricing** — taxes and known fees included; no end-of-checkout surprises.
4. **Honest ranking** — we never sell placement; results are ranked by real guest quality, not who pays us.
5. **Your data is not the product** — we don't profile you to set a price.

(Caveat to stay exactly-true: we control *our* fee and *our* ranking — not the hotel's base rate, which
still moves with supply/demand. Never claim "always lowest" or "no dynamic pricing anywhere.")

## 3. Content strategy — brand-first, before generic travel
Lead with content **only we can write** (an OTA can't honestly publish "how OTAs hide markups" — conflict
of interest). This is simultaneously brand, trust, PR, and SEO-moat content. Generic destination content
comes *after* this foundation.

**Initial set (build these first):**
- **The Charter / manifesto** — "Our honest-travel pledge." The anchor page.
- **"How we make money"** — radical transparency about our own model. (OTAs never publish this — that's the point.)
- **Exposé/explainers** (each targets a real, high-trust query the incumbents avoid):
  - "Do hotel prices change based on your phone or location?"
  - "What a 'resort fee' really is — and why your deal isn't one"
  - "How do hotel booking sites actually make money?"
  - "Same room, different price: how personalized pricing works"
- **"Don't trust us — test it."** The surveillance claim is *testable*: open our price on two phones — it's
  identical. A killer, shareable build-in-public demo (video + page).

**Guardrails for exposés:** frame as *industry practice* + cite research; never make a false, specific
accusation about a named competitor (defamation risk). Attack the practice, not the company.

**Why it compounds:** these pieces rank for trust queries, earn links/PR (a stance is linkable), convert
better (trust → booking), and feed the campaign — all before we spend a dollar on generic guides.

## 4. Monetization — transparency is the business model, not a constraint
The trap is thinking honesty limits revenue. The opposite: the **right** model *is* the product and the
marketing. Pick models where **our incentive is aligned with the traveler's**, and **publish them**.

**Phase 1 — now (cash flow, no liability):** affiliate referral, **fully disclosed**.
- Revenue = referral commission paid by the hotel/partner — **it never changes your price**.
- **We never sell ranking** (commitment #4) and **never personalize price** (#1). That neutrality is itself
  the differentiator vs OTAs that auction placement.
- Plus the Viator "Things to do" affiliate (already live, disclosed, `rel=sponsored`).

**Phase 2 — the moat (the true model):** become merchant of record → **cost-plus + flat fee**, same for
everyone; free sign-in unlocks below-SSP pricing (LiteAPI permits below-SSP behind a login). Needs
Seller-of-Travel + payments + the pricing engine (flagged in POSITIONING). Revenue = the **flat fee** (we
can literally state it), optionally a **membership**.

**The Costco frame (the story that sells the model):** *"We don't profit from a bigger markup — we profit
from a small flat fee / membership. So we have zero reason to inflate your price. We're on your side."*
Aligned incentives, stated out loud. That sentence is both the revenue model and the ad.

**Always:** disclose every revenue source in plain language (keep `/disclosure` prominent); never sell
ranking; never personalize price; be willing to publish the fee.

## 5. The flywheel
Transparency content → trust + links + PR → bookings → (membership) revenue → more content + proof.
Each turn widens the gap the incumbents can't cross.
