# Stories & Anecdotes

> The story bank for blog posts and copy. Two kinds: **real Hawaii stories/legends** (true and
> citable — use freely) and **brand stories** (our own, true-to-life — personalize, never fabricate).
>
> **Rules (from `voice.md`):** at most **one story per post**; always anchor it with **one concrete,
> true detail**; and **never invent** a fake customer, name, number, or event. If nothing fits, write
> the post without a story rather than making one up.

## A. Real Hawaii stories & legends (true, citable — the workhorses)

These are genuine Hawaiian history/mythology. They make great post openers and the Kennedy "borrowed
authority" device. Link the source when you state a fact that could be challenged.

- **Hina at Rainbow Falls.** Legend says the lava cave behind Rainbow Falls (Waiānuenue) in Hilo is
  the home of Hina, goddess of the moon and mother of the demigod Māui, who was tormented by a moʻo
  (dragon-like water spirit) in the river. → *waterfalls, Hilo, Hawaiian mythology.*
- **Māui snared the sun on Haleakalā.** "Haleakalā" means "house of the sun" — Māui is said to have
  lassoed the sun's rays from the summit to slow it down so his mother Hina could dry her kapa. →
  *Haleakalā sunrise, Maui, mythology.*
- **Pele lives in Halemaʻumaʻu.** Pele, goddess of fire and volcanoes, is said to dwell in the
  Halemaʻumaʻu crater of Kīlauea — which is exactly why offerings and respect matter there. →
  *Hawaii Volcanoes NP, Big Island, respect/culture.*
- **The shaka and Hamana Kalili.** The most-told origin: Kalili of Lāʻie lost the three middle fingers
  of his right hand in a Kahuku sugar-mill accident, became the sugar-train guard, and his thumb-and-
  pinky "all clear" wave was copied by kids into the shaka. → *shaka, Oahu, aloha spirit.*
- **The Battle of Kepaniwai (1790).** In ʻĪao Valley, Kamehameha's forces (with Western cannons)
  defeated Maui's army so decisively that fallen warriors were said to dam ʻĪao Stream — "Kepaniwai,"
  the damming of the waters. Part of his island unification. → *Iao Valley, Kamehameha, history.*
- **Kamehameha at the Nuʻuanu Pali.** In the 1795 battle, Oahu's warriors were driven off the ~1,000-ft
  cliffs, delivering Oahu to Kamehameha. → *Nuʻuanu Pali, Oahu, unification.*
- **Kalākaua, the "Merrie Monarch."** King David Kalākaua revived hula publicly in the 1880s after its
  1830 ban, calling it "the language of the heart." The Merrie Monarch Festival in Hilo is named for
  him. → *hula, Hawaiian culture, history.*
- **Loco moco was born in Hilo, 1949.** Created at the Lincoln Grill (now Cafe 100) for local teens who
  wanted something cheap, fast, and filling. → *loco moco, local food.*
- **Spam musubi's WWII roots.** Spam arrived in the Pacific by the millions of cans in WWII and stuck;
  the modern musubi is widely credited to Barbara Funamura of Kauai (~1982). → *spam musubi, food culture.*

*(Reminder: these are the ones we already use across the blog — keep them consistent post to post.)*

## B. Brand stories (ours — true-to-life; personalize, never fabricate)

We don't have a deep archive of named customer stories yet, and we **never invent one** (no fake
"Sarah from Kailua"). Use these honest, recurring, true-to-our-business patterns instead — and when a
post needs a specific detail, **ask the owner for a real one** rather than making it up.

- **The origin story.** *(OWNER TO PROVIDE — do not invent.)* Why Hawaii Picnics & Beach Events started,
  the first beach, where the name "Wember" comes from, the first proposal or picnic you set up. One
  real, specific detail makes the whole brand feel human. → *About page, trust, "why we do this."*
- **We take the weather risk, not you.** Our real policy: free reschedule if severe rain is forecast —
  we'd rather move your date than put you on a windblown beach for the sake of a booking. → *trust,
  "is it worth it," planning.*
- **The "you can DIY this" conversation.** We genuinely tell people to skip us when a cooler and a
  sunset will do. We'd rather earn trust than oversell a setup you don't need. → *the "when not to
  hire us" tell, transparency.*
- **The permit dance.** Oahu beaches need DLNR shoreline / city park permits; we pull them so your
  golden hour isn't interrupted by a ranger asking for paperwork. → *what's included, why use a vendor.*
- **Sunset proposals, the pattern (not a named couple).** The rehearsed speech that evaporates, the
  shaking hands, the "this is the part we'll actually remember" afterward — described as the kind of
  moment we set the stage for, never as a fabricated specific person. → *proposals, the brand promise.*
- **"The sun has never once been late."** The brand's own dry line about the one thing we can promise
  at a sunset picnic. → *a sign-off flavor; use sparingly.*

## How to use these

- **One story per post, max.** More feels like stuffing.
- **Anchor it with one true, concrete detail** — a place, a real policy, a real date/fact from the
  legend. Concreteness is what makes it land.
- **Section A is true history** — use freely, cite the source for any contestable fact.
- **Section B is ours** — keep it honest. Personalize from the owner's real experience; **never
  invent** a customer, a number, or an event. If you need a specific and don't have a real one, leave
  the story out.
- **Never aim a story at the expense of Hawaiian culture or locals** (see `Humour.md` / `Opinions.md`).
