## On planning a Hawaii trip

- **For a week or less, stay on one island.** Each inter-island hop burns the better part of a day
  in airport time, so a 7-day, two-island trip is really two rushed 3-day trips with a travel day
  stapled in the middle. One island, no airport days, sees more and stresses less.
- **Pick a few big experiences and let the island carry the rest.** The classic first-timer mistake
  is a 12-stop daily itinerary; Hawaii punishes over-scheduling by trapping you in the car. Choose
  two or three headliners and leave room to do nothing.
- **The coast matters more than the month.** Hawaii's leeward (south/west) sides are sunny and dry
  year-round; windward (east) sides are green and rainy. "What's the weather in June?" is the wrong
  question — "which side am I on?" is the right one.
- **Don't pack the first morning.** Mainland jet lag works *for* you at sunrise and *against* you at
  night, so lean into early starts and early dinners for the first couple of days.

## On where the money's worth it (and what's free)

- **The best things in Hawaii are free: the beaches, the sunsets, the hikes, the ocean.** A perfect
  Hawaii day can cost nothing. Spend your money only on the few things you genuinely can't DIY.
- **Pay for a tour only when you can't reach it from shore.** Molokini, the Na Pali Coast, the
  Mauna Kea summit, the Big Island manta dive — yes. A reef you can swim to off the sand — no, snorkel
  it for free.
- **A guided Mauna Kea tour earns its ~$250 for most people**, because it solves the 4WD road, the
  13,800-foot altitude, and the parkas in one evening — and the summit closes after dark anyway, so
  DIY only really gets you the sunset.
- **Eat the humble version, skip the gourmet reinvention.** The $10 diner loco moco beats the $26
  wagyu one; the busy plate-lunch counter beats the resort dining room. This is soul food — it
  doesn't need improving.

## On food

- **Eat where locals line up, not where the resort points you.** Musubi from a 7-Eleven, poke from a
  grocery counter, a plate lunch from a drive-in — a few dollars each, and the realest food on the
  islands.
- **The detail is the whole point.** A spam musubi without the shoyu-sugar glaze is a salty brick; a
  loco moco needs the runny sunny-side-up egg. The cheap stuff is great *because* it's done right,
  not in spite of being cheap.
- **Try poi at least once** — fresh, and as a side with something salty, not judged by a cold spoonful
  on its own. (Then decide. Plenty of people don't love it, and that's fair.)

## On respecting Hawaii (non-negotiable — no jokes here)

- **The ocean is the real danger, not sharks.** Drownings, not bites, are the actual risk. Heed
  lifeguards and warning flags, never turn your back on the waves, and stay out of the water at
  unguarded or posted-dangerous spots (Queen's Bath, high-surf shorelines).
- **Reef-safe mineral sunscreen isn't a suggestion — it's the law.** Hawaii bans oxybenzone/
  octinoxate sunscreens. Wear mineral, or cover up with a rash guard.
- **Never touch the turtles or monk seals.** They're protected; keep 10+ feet back. The photo isn't
  worth a fine or the harm.
- **Sacred is sacred.** Heiau, Mauna Kea, ʻĪao, Pele's home — don't stack rocks, hop barriers, or use
  ceremony as a backdrop. Visit with humility; it's a living culture, not a theme park.
- **Hula is storytelling, not a hip-shaking floor show — watch the hands.** Same spirit with the
  shaka: use it sincerely, or not at all.

## On islands & where to go

- **There's no single "best island" — it depends on what you want.** Oahu is the all-rounder
  (beaches, city, history, the famous surf, easiest logistics); Maui is beaches-and-romance; Kauai is
  the wild, green one; the Big Island is the adventurer's island, with an active volcano and the best
  stargazing in the country.
- **First trip? Make it Oahu.** The widest range and the easiest logistics — you can do a lot without
  a marathon drive.
- **Don't cross the Big Island for one stop.** It's bigger than all the other islands combined; base
  near what you came for (Hilo side for the volcano and waterfalls, Kona side for sun and snorkeling).

## On "is it worth it?" (honest verdicts)

- **Worth it:** a sunrise, a Na Pali boat trip, Pearl Harbor, one good luau, the manta dive,
  Hawaii Volcanoes National Park.
- **Worth it, but manage expectations:** Rainbow Falls (stunning for near-zero effort, but the
  rainbow needs sun *and* morning timing), the Road to Hana (it's the journey, not one payoff),
  Waikiki nightlife (sunset mai tais and live music, not Vegas — and that's the point).
- **Skip it if you're short on time:** the long detour for a single roadside photo, and the packed
  bus tour that gives you 20 minutes a stop. ʻĪao Valley is a 20-minute stop, not a half-day — pair
  it with Central Maui rather than making a special trip.

## How to use opinions in content

- **One strong opinion per post, maximum.** Drop it where it makes the post feel human instead of
  generic — usually the "is it worth it" / "here's the honest call" beat.
- **Always back it with a number or a fact.** "Mauna Kea tours are worth it" is a bumper sticker;
  "they solve the 4WD road, the 13,800-ft altitude, and the parkas for ~$250, and the summit closes
  after dark anyway" is an opinion.
- **Tell people when NOT to do/buy/hire — including us.** The biggest voice tell. We'll talk someone
  out of a tour they can do free from the sand, or out of hiring us when a DIY cooler will do.
- **Never aim the hot take at Hawaiian culture or locals.** Punch at tourist clichés, overpriced
  tours, and ourselves — never at the islands' people or traditions.
