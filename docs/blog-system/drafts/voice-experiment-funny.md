## DIAL 6 — dry-plus (current voice, just more of it)

### Do you actually need sunscreen in Hawaii?

Yes. More than you think, and earlier than you think.

Hawaii sits about 21 degrees off the equator, so the sun here isn't the gentle mainland sun you negotiate with — it's the sun with something to prove. People who "don't really burn" at home discover, around hour two on a Waikiki beach, that they very much do. The clouds lie to you. So does the breeze. You feel cool and look like a stop sign by dinner.

Buy reef-safe — no oxybenzone or octinoxate, both banned here since 2021, because the reef was here first. SPF 30 minimum, reapply every two hours and after every swim.

I used to skip sunscreen. It grew on me. Mostly across the one patch of shoulder I always miss.

The honest version: the cheap sunscreen you actually reapply beats the fancy one you used once and left in the rental car to become soup.

## DIAL 8 — dad-joke-forward (jokes spread around, still useful)

### Is Spam actually good, or is that just a Hawaii thing?

It's good. Genuinely. Drop the mainland baggage at the gate.

Hawaii eats more Spam per capita than any state in the country — somewhere around 5 to 7 million cans a year. That's not irony. Spam musubi — a slab of grilled, glazed Spam belted onto warm rice with a strip of nori — is the perfect $3 thing to eat with one hand while the other hand holds a shave ice you ordered two sizes too big.

Why here? Wartime rationing made canned meat a staple, and locals did what locals do: made it taste good instead of complaining about it.

My qualifications, for the record: I am not a food critic. I run beach picnics and have eaten Spam musubi for breakfast more times than I'll admit to a doctor.

What do you call Spam that does stand-up? Canned laughter.

Try it once from a real local counter before you decide. Worst case you're out three dollars and one strongly held opinion.

## DIAL 9 — full groan (dad jokes doing real work)

### Should you drive the Road to Hana?

Maybe. It depends how you feel about 600-some curves and 50-odd one-lane bridges standing between you and a waterfall.

The Road to Hana is about 64 miles of coastline folded up like origami and handed to a road engineer with a sense of humour. It's 2.5 to 3 hours one way with no stops — and the stops are the entire point, so block out a full day.

A few honest truths. The passenger who swore they "don't get carsick" is, statistically, lying — they just haven't met curve 200 yet. Bring a bag. Bring snacks. Bring patience, because at some point you will be stuck behind someone doing 12 miles an hour to honor a tree.

I had a great joke about the one-lane bridges. I'll wait my turn.

And the waterfalls are genuinely a step up — mostly because you take about four hundred steps to reach them.

We run picnics on Oahu, not Maui, so I've got no horse in this race, which means I can be straight with you: if your group gets queasy or your trip is short, this drive is less vacation, more endurance event. But the waterfalls are real. So is the banana bread.

## DIAL 9 — round 2 (keep calibrating)

### Is the pineapple in Hawaii actually from Hawaii?

Mostly, no. Sorry to be the one to break it.

Hawaii was the pineapple capital of the world for most of the last century — James Dole turned the island of Lanai into one giant plantation, and "Pineapple Island" stuck. But big commercial growing moved to cheaper labor markets decades ago, so the pineapple in your hotel fruit bowl probably commuted in from Costa Rica or the Philippines.

The good news: you can still get the real thing. Maui Gold still grows on the slopes of Haleakala, and it's sweeter than anything that survives a cargo ship. Buy one at a farm stand, not the airport.

I told my kid a pineapple has no feelings. She asked how I could be sure. I said look at the crown — it always looks like it's taking the news well.

And yes, it belongs on pizza. We run beach picnics, not a pizzeria, so that's a free opinion with no money behind it. Fight your family about it on your own time.

### When can you see whales in Hawaii?

Roughly November through May, with January to March the peak — busy season for the whales and the boats both.

Humpbacks (koholā) swim down from Alaska every winter to breed and calve in the warm, shallow water between the islands. Thousands make the trip. They don't call ahead, so nothing is guaranteed, but in peak season you can often spot a spout from shore with no boat at all.

Bring binoculars. Bring motion-sickness pills if you take a boat, because the same swells the whales love will introduce you to your breakfast a second time.

What do you call a group of humpbacks playing together? An orca-stra. I know orcas aren't humpbacks. The whales let it slide.

We set up sunset picnics on Oahu, and from the right beach in February you can watch for spouts with something cold in hand — which beats a rocking boat for some people and misses the splash for others. Your call.

### What's the catch with a beach picnic?

Sand. The catch is always sand. We run a beach picnic company, so we've made peace with our nemesis.

The honest pitch: we set up a styled sunset picnic on an Oahu beach — low table, cushions, real plates, food, flowers, the whole thing — from $349 for two. You show up, sit down, and watch the sun do the one trick it's truly good at. We handle setup and teardown. You don't haul a cooler or wrestle a folding chair that has clearly wrestled before.

What we cannot defeat is sand. Sand is nature's glitter: it gets everywhere, it stays for weeks, and you'll find some in a bag in October and have no idea which beach it's from. We keep it to a minimum. We do not promise victory. Nobody can.

And we only do Oahu — so if your trip is Maui or Kauai, we'll happily tell you we're the wrong people and send you off with a wave. Honesty is cheaper than sand, and a lot easier to get out of your shoes.
