# How a blog post gets written and published — the simple version

This is the plain-English version of what happens when you say *"write and publish a blog post."*
No code words. Think of it like a recipe — each step has a "what" and a "why," and a box to tick.

---

## Part 1 — Figure out what to write about

- [ ] **1. Pick a topic people actually Google.** We look at a big list of real Hawaii searches and
      pick one that (a) lots of people search, (b) isn't too hard to win, and (c) we haven't already
      written about. *Why: writing about things nobody searches = nobody reads it.*
- [ ] **2. Google the topic and study the winners.** We open the top few articles already ranking
      and see how long they are and what they cover. We aim to be **a little better and a little
      longer than the best one** — never shorter. *Why: Google ranks the most complete answer.*

## Part 2 — Write it well

- [ ] **3. Answer the question in the very first line** (in bold), then say something a little funny
      or surprising so people keep reading. *Why: people decide in 3 seconds whether to stay.*
- [ ] **4. Write in our voice** — helpful, honest, a bit funny, like a local friend giving the real
      scoop (the lot that fills up by 7am, where the locals actually go). *Why: real, first-hand tips
      are what Google rewards and people share.*
- [ ] **5. Keep paragraphs short** and add a clear menu (table of contents) at the top.
- [ ] **6. Put the leftover little questions in an FAQ at the bottom** — but ONLY questions the post
      didn't already answer up top. *Why: repeating yourself wastes the reader's time and Google notices.*

## Part 3 — Make it look good (without slowing it down)

- [ ] **7. Keep ONE cover photo** (the little picture you see on the blog list and when it's shared).
- [ ] **8. Use our colorful cards, charts, and timelines instead of stock photos** in the body.
      *Why: they're on-brand, load fast, never look generic, and Google can actually read them.*

## Part 4 — Make it earn money (gently)

- [ ] **9. Add helpful affiliate links** where they fit — tours, hotels, gear we'd genuinely
      recommend (we earn a small cut, you pay nothing extra). Plus **one quiet mention** of our
      picnic service. *Why: the blog pays for itself, but help first — never salesy.*

## Part 5 — Put it on the website and check everything

- [ ] **10. Add the post to the site.**
- [ ] **11. Run the full checklist** (`BlogPostChecklist.md`) — is the title the right length, do all
      the links work, does it load fast, is the data correct, no robotic-sounding sentences?
- [ ] **12. Run the speed/quality test (Lighthouse).** It scores the page out of 100 on speed,
      SEO, accessibility, and best-practices. **It has to score at least 95 / 100 / 100 / 100** or it
      does NOT go live. *Why: a slow or broken page hurts rankings.*

## Part 6 — Go live and tell Google

- [ ] **13. If everything passed, publish it live** (it deploys to the website automatically).
- [ ] **14. Tell Google to come look** (Search Console → "Request Indexing") so it shows up in days,
      not weeks.

## Later — keep it fresh (this is the new part)

- [ ] **15. Come back to old posts every so often** and refresh them — fix dead links, update the
      year and any prices, add anything new people are searching. *Why: on a content site, updating
      old posts is often more valuable than writing brand-new ones.*

---

**The one rule that overrides everything:** if step 11 or 12 isn't fully green, it does **not** get
published. Better to fix it than ship something broken.

*(Want it held back to review before it goes live? Just say "draft only" — then it's written and
saved but NOT published, waiting for your thumbs-up.)*
