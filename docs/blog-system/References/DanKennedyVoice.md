## TL;DR — our voice in one sentence

A plain-spoken local who answers your question in the first sentence, backs every claim with a real
number, tells you straight when something isn't worth it, and quietly points you to one useful next
step — never a pitch.

---

## 1. Core voice traits

**Direct, second-person, conversational.** Write *to* "you," like a friend who actually lives here,
not a brochure. "You" and "we"/"I" freely; contractions always. *Advice over coffee, not a podium.*

**Confident, but it earns it with proof.** Make the call, then back it with a real number or fact.
That's exactly our **one strong opinion per post** (from `opinions.md`), backed by a figure from
`stats.md`. Pattern: **claim → proof → why it matters to you.**
- "The $10 diner loco moco beats the $26 gourmet reinvention. This is soul food; it doesn't need improving."
- "For most visitors, a guided Mauna Kea tour is the smart call — it solves the 4WD, the 13,800-ft altitude, and the parkas in one go."

**Anti-fluff, anti-pretension.** Plain words, plain logic. No jargon, no AI-tells, no throat-clearing.
This is the same list `voice.md` bans ("unlock," "seamless," "world-class," "in today's…").

**Honest to a fault — that's the trust engine.** Kennedy lowers his own status to raise the reader's
belief ("I'm a high-school graduate"). Our version is stronger and on-brand: **tell people when NOT
to do/buy/hire** — the single biggest thing that makes us sound real.
- "Rainbow Falls isn't worth a special two-hour drive from the Kona side — it's a Hilo-day stop."
- "We run beach picnics on Oahu, not the Big Island, so we have no stake in your plans."

**Wry, dry humor as seasoning.** A light parenthetical aside, never a comedy routine (see `humour.md`).
One wry line per few hundred words, and it never derails the point.

## 2. Sentence & paragraph mechanics

- **Short paragraphs.** 2–4 sentences; hard cap **~700 characters / ~150 words** (BlogStructure rule).
  White space is a tool.
- **Vary sentence length on purpose.** A long, rolling explanation, then a short line that lands.
- **One-line declarations for emphasis — but no exclamation marks.** Kennedy used "Demonstrate the
  value!"; we get the same punch with a period or italics. *The glaze is the whole point.* / "Watch
  the hands, not the hips." Emphasis comes from brevity and placement, never from `!` or ALL-CAPS.
- **Em-dashes and parentheticals** carry the asides and the dry jokes.
- **Rhetorical questions** to pivot: "Worth the hype? Mostly."
- **Direct, imperative verbs** to start sentences: "Go early." "Book the slot first." "Skip the
  bare-bones hotel-lawn version."

## 3. Structural patterns (mapped to our anatomy)

- **Numbered/stepped lists for any process** → our `steps` infographic and "how to make/visit X"
  sections. Break method into countable, do-able chunks.
- **Benefit-driven subheads, not dry labels.** Our H2s should promise a payoff. Prefer "How to see
  the rainbow" over "Overview," "Is it worth it?" over "Conclusion."
- **Proof through specifics — real numbers, named places.** Vague claims are death. "$5 per person
  plus $10 per vehicle," "80-foot waterfall," "from $349 for two," "5–7 million cans a year." Pull
  pricing/jobs/response-time/review figures from `stats.md`; never invent or round.
- **Borrowed authority** → our **verified external links** (.gov/.edu/major) and named sources
  (DLNR, NOAA, the Polynesian Cultural Center), plus a quotable one-liner where it's truthful.
- **The "yes-momentum" close, softened.** Build the useful case, then point at **one** clear next
  step — for us that's a relevant Viator tour, an Expedia "compare hotels," or the picnic — offered,
  not pushed. One CTA is plenty.

## 4. Signature devices (steal these — in our register)

| Device | Our version |
|---|---|
| Bold claim + instant proof | The one strong opinion, backed by a `stats.md` number ("the glaze is non-negotiable… skeptics convert on the first bite"). |
| "Tell them when not to" as credibility | The honest caveat — what to skip, when it's not worth it, who shouldn't go. Our biggest trust signal. |
| The disarming aside | A dry parenthetical to keep it human (used sparingly). |
| Quotable maxim | A short, true line set on its own: "Watch the hands, not the hips." "Use it sincerely, or not at all." |
| Standalone punch line | After a long explanation, one short sentence — no exclamation mark. |
| Benefit-driven subhead | H2s that promise a payoff, never just name a topic. |
| One clear next step | A single soft CTA (tour / hotels / picnic) at the natural end, after the value is delivered. |

## 5. What to avoid (reinforces `voice.md`)

- **No corporate jargon / buzzwords.** No "leverage," "seamless," "world-class," "best-in-class."
- **No hedging.** Skip "perhaps," "it could be argued." State the caveat plainly and move on.
- **No throat-clearing intros.** Answer the query in sentence one (also the featured-snippet play).
- **No fluff adjectives.** Cut "very/really/incredibly." Specifics beat intensifiers.
- **Don't bury the next step**, and never end on "hopefully this helps."
- **Don't over-joke.** Humor is seasoning.
- **And, per voice.md and the brand:** no exclamation marks, no emoji, no ALL-CAPS, no hard-sell
  urgency, no invented or rounded numbers.

---

## 6. Calibration for us (important)

Kennedy's job was to make the sale *on the page*; ours is to be **the most useful, honest guide on
the topic**, earn trust, and convert softly.

- **Keep:** directness, "you," confident proof-with-specifics, numbered steps, benefit-driven
  subheads, dry one-line punches, the always-point-to-one-next-step instinct.
- **Drop:** ALL-CAPS, exclamation points, "ACT NOW / book today," manufactured urgency, multiple CTAs.

The next step is **earned, not demanded**: we've genuinely helped, so a single relevant tour, hotel
comparison, or the Oahu picnic at the end feels like a service, not a pitch. Our honesty — especially
telling people when to skip something — is what makes the soft sell land.

Think *"a straight-talking Oahu local explaining this properly on their blog,"* not *"a sales letter."*

## 7. Before / after (a Hawaii example)

**Generic / off-voice:**
> Mauna Kea is widely regarded as one of the world's premier stargazing destinations, offering
> visitors an unforgettable, world-class experience beneath truly breathtaking skies.

**In our voice:**
> Mauna Kea has some of the darkest, clearest skies on Earth — and one catch most guides gloss over:
> the summit closes after dark, so night stargazing happens lower down or on a tour. At 13,800 feet,
> with a 4WD-only road and near-freezing cold, a guided tour is the smart call for most people. Here's
> how to do it right.
