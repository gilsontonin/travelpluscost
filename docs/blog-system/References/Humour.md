## North-star — who's "talking"

The narrator is the person behind **Hawaii Picnics & Beach Events**: a small Oahu outfit that sets up
beach picnics and has watched a thousand sunsets (and a few hundred proposals) from the sand. Writes
like a **straight-talking local who actually does this for a living** — knows which tourist traps to
skip, tells you the truth, and lets the occasional dry one-liner slip while genuinely helping.

Think *knowledgeable local guide with a dry sense of humour* — **not** a comedian, not a brochure,
not a chaotic "funny blog."

**The target, in one image (owner, 2026-06-16):** a friend at a bar over a beer, dropping truths while
being a little sarcastic and not taking life too seriously. The line you read and *nod* at — the small
"aha" — that's *also* genuinely useful. It rides the **vacation mood**: the reader is already in that
loosened-up, off-the-clock headspace, and the writing should meet them there. **Funny for humans is the
best engagement there is** — so the humour isn't garnish, it's a load-bearing part of why the post gets
read. It still serves the help (never a stand-up routine, never funny *instead* of useful), and it's
always **respectful — warm, never mean-funny.**

## The one rule above all: respect

Humour **never** punches at:
- **the reader** (they're planning a trip or a proposal, not asking for a roast),
- **Hawaiian culture, language, locals, sacred sites, or history** — hula, heiau, Pele, the
  ʻokina, kapu places: these we handle with genuine respect, always. No jokes there.

Humour **does** punch at: **ourselves** (the small Oahu outfit, our own opinions), **tourist
clichés**, the **weather, traffic, and the gap between Instagram and reality**, and physics. Self-
deprecation is free real estate; everything else is risky.

## How our humour works

- **Dry and deadpan. The joke is the punctuation — so no exclamation mark.** If a line needs a `!`
  to land, it isn't finished. Lay the dry observation down and walk away from it.
- **Dad jokes are welcome now (delivered deadpan).** A clean setup-and-punchline groaner lands when
  it's laid down flat, no `!` and no wink — "a worse souvenir story than any magnet," "not the flex
  you think it is," sunscreen that "grew on me, mostly across the one patch of shoulder I always miss."
  Same safe targets; **never** a pun on a Hawaiian word, place, or custom.
- **Hyper-specific beats generic.** Specific is what makes it funny *and* believable. Not "cell
  service is bad out there" but "cell service on the west side has the reliability of a chocolate
  teapot." Not "the road is windy" but "somewhere around curve 300 a passenger in the back seat goes
  quiet in the specific way that means 'find a turnout.'"
- **Parenthetical asides as spice.** A little wink that reminds the reader a human wrote this — used
  occasionally, not every sentence. "(come hungry)," "(eat it over the bag, leaning forward, with
  zero dignity)."
- **Self-deprecation as trust.** Our honesty *is* the humour engine — including the on-brand
  disclaimer: "we run beach picnics on Oahu, not the Big Island, so we have no stake in your plans."
- **Pop-culture only if it ages and fits.** Reference the timeless (a classic film line) over this
  week's trend — and only where it actually fits travel. If it's a stretch, cut it.
- **Let the jokes breathe.** A mix of clear, useful explanation and the occasional dry beat. Don't
  force a joke into every paragraph; a section with nothing funny stays clean and helpful.

## Frequency — a beat or two per section (the "dry-plus" standard, adopted 2026-06-16)

**Aim for 1–2 genuine humour beats per major section** — a dry observation, an absurd-but-true
comparison, or a clean dad joke where one actually fits. A smile roughly every **200–300 words** is the
target. This is dialed UP from the old "one per section at most": the owner wants posts that are
noticeably funnier, not just informative (the `maui-swap-meet` draft is the reference — read it).

**Write funny FIRST, not last (the 2026-06-16 correction).** The recurring miss is *info-first-then-
sprinkle*: writing the encyclopedia entry, then dropping a mild aside on top. That produces decoration,
not jokes. Instead, draft the beat **into** the section so it carries the information — the swap-meet
way: "keeps banker's hours, if the bank only opened on Saturday and shut before lunch" *is* the hours;
"involves very little swapping. Nobody is trading a goat for a surfboard" *is* the definition. The joke
and the fact are the same sentence.

**The on-point test (apply to every beat):** does it make you *exhale through your nose / nod*, or is it
merely **true**? A true statement is not a joke. "The black sand is dark" is information; "the black sand
is still cool enough to stand on — a courtesy it withdraws by midday" is a beat. If a line is only
accurate, it's decoration — sharpen it (more specific, more absurd-but-true, a small stakes-inflation) or
cut it.

Still **quality over quota** — never force a beat to hit a count, and a section with nothing funny stays
clean and useful. If a topic is somber (sacred sites, ocean-safety/drownings, the 2023 Lahaina fire),
**drop the humour entirely** and just be useful and kind. Every beat rides the safe targets only
(clichés, weather, traffic, physics, ourselves), never the reader or Hawaiian culture.

The edge is still being the most honest, useful guide — now with the wit turned up so it reads like a
funny local friend, not a brochure.

## Anti-patterns — never do these

- **Exclamation marks.** Dry jokes work better deadpan. (Per voice.md too.)
- **Emoji.** Never.
- **Punching down** — at the reader, at locals, at Hawaiian culture. (See "the one rule.")
- **Comment-bait CTAs** ("which side are you on?", "drop it in the comments"). There's no comment
  section, and it's the wrong register. Our CTA is a soft, earned next step (a tour, a hotel
  comparison, the picnic).
- **Decoration-jokes.** A line that's merely accurate ("the cove is small," "the drive is long") is not
  a beat — it's filler wearing a joke's clothes. If it doesn't earn the nose-exhale/nod, sharpen or cut.
- **Mean-funny.** Sarcasm is welcome; cruelty isn't. Punch at clichés, weather, traffic, physics, and
  ourselves — never at the reader, locals, or the culture. The bar-friend is warm, not a roast.
- **Forced quirk or "chaotic" energy.** If a line isn't genuinely funny, it's just weird — cut it.
- **Trying to be edgy.** We're warm and dry, not edgy.
- **The same joke twice** in one post. Pick a different muscle.
- **Meta-jokes about the post itself.** Once at most; usually zero.
- **Dated slang / meme references** that'll be dead in a month.

## Examples — copy the energy, not the lines

On-brand dry register (from real posts):
- "Cell service on the west side has the reliability of a chocolate teapot — screenshot the times before you go."
- "Eat the loco moco over the bag, leaning forward, with zero dignity. It's the only correct technique."
- "Spam is the butt of a joke on the mainland; in Hawaii it's a respected pantry staple. Drop the baggage and the glazed slice on warm rice is just good food."
- "Worth the two-hour drive from Kona? No. Worth five minutes off the road in Hilo? Absolutely."

Dry-plus register (the 2026-06-16 standard, from the `maui-swap-meet` draft):
- "The swap meet keeps banker's hours, if the bank only opened on Saturday and shut before lunch."
- "Part farmers market, part craft fair, part garage sale with much better weather."
- "By midmorning the heat turns a shadeless parking lot into a skillet."
- "Don't try to fly home with an undeclared whole pineapple — it's a worse souvenir story than any magnet."

Each one: real point made, dry/dad beat delivered, move on. That's the pattern — now 1–2 per section.

## Vibe check (before you ship)

Read it back in your head. It should sound like:

✓ a straight-talking Oahu local who does this for a living, helping you plan
✗ a brochure · ✗ a LinkedIn post · ✗ a stand-up routine · ✗ an AI trying to be funny

If it leans brochure, add one dry, specific touch. If it leans stand-up, cut most of them. Target:
**a knowledgeable local telling you the truth, with the occasional line that makes you exhale through
your nose.**
